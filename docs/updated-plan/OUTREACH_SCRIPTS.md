# BAAM Review — Founding Customer Outreach Scripts

**Version:** 1.0
**Date:** May 12, 2026
**Purpose:** Templates for the three outreach tiers, in English and 中文, plus rebuttal scripts for the most common objections.

---

## How to use this document

These are **starting points**, not scripts you read verbatim. Every conversation with a small business owner is a relationship — they can tell when you're reading. Customize the opener with something specific you know about their business ("I noticed you got three reviews last week, all five stars"). Use the templates for structure, not for words.

The 中文 versions are written in **conversational mainland-style** appropriate for first-generation immigrant SMB owners. They are slightly more formal than the EN versions because the WeChat / phone context calls for it. If your customer is Cantonese-speaking primarily, adjust accordingly — the templates use Mandarin idiom.

Three sections:
1. **Tier 1 — Warmest (existing BAAM Studio clients)** — Already trust you. The ask is small.
2. **Tier 2 — Warm (cold-but-validated)** — Don't know you yet but you have a credible angle. The ask is larger.
3. **Tier 3 — Cold (broader category)** — Generic outreach. Lowest conversion, requires the most polish.

Plus a fourth section: **Rebuttals** for the 8 most common objections.

## TIER 1 — Existing BAAM Studio Clients

These are your warmest leads. Most of them will say yes within 24 hours if you ask properly. The mistake to avoid is treating this like a sales pitch — they don't need to be sold, they need to be invited.

**EN:**

**中文：**

> 再提一下，不急。如果你想先看一下大概是什么样的，我可以发你一个三分钟的演示视频。或者你就回一句"下个月再说"，我下个月再找你。

### Tier 1 · The screen-share onboarding flow

When they say yes, schedule a 30-minute screen share. Use this as your structure (not a script):

1. **Minutes 0–5:** Show the marketing landing page. Don't oversell — they already trust you. Just orient them. "This is what your customers will see when they get the SMS. This is what you'll see on the back end."

2. **Minutes 5–15:** Walk through their actual signup. Have them do it on screen-share, with you watching. They create their account, set up their first location (their business name + address + Google Business Profile link), customize the brand color and logo. Make sure their location is actually verified on Google before continuing.

3. **Minutes 15–25:** Send the first review request together. Pick a real recent customer they actually know. Send it to their own phone first so they can see the customer experience. Then send a real one to a real customer.

4. **Minutes 25–30:** Show them where to find things later — the dashboard, the AI Reply queue, the revenue attribution number. Point at the revenue attribution number specifically. Say: "This is the number you should check once a week. It tells you whether BAAM Review is worth its rent."

5. **End of call:** Confirm WeChat is the right way to reach you for any issue. Add them to a private "BAAM Review Founders" WeChat group (or equivalent) that gives them a peer cohort.

These are people who don't know you yet, but you have a credible angle: their business already cares about reviews (4.5★+ rating, 30+ reviews on Google), they serve the Chinese-speaking community in NY metro, or they were referred by a BAAM Studio client. The ask is larger because the trust is thinner.

## REBUTTALS — for the 8 most common objections

These come up over and over. The rebuttals below are calibrated to be honest first, persuasive second. Don't fake-friendly your way past someone's real concern.

### Objection 1: "I already use Birdeye / Podium / NiceJob"

**EN:**

> Totally fair — those are solid tools, especially Birdeye. The main differences:
>
> - Birdeye is $299–$599/month with a $500–$1,500 setup fee and a sales contract. We're $89/month founding, no setup, no contract.
> - Birdeye and Podium are English-first; Spanish is translated; Chinese is rarely supported well. We were built for trilingual operation from day one.
> - Most importantly, those tools stop at "collect the review." BAAM Review keeps going — display widgets, social distribution, share-with-a-friend referrals, revenue attribution. That's why our customers stick around even when Birdeye is offering them 50% off to come back.
>
> No pressure — if Birdeye is working and the price isn't an issue, stay. But if you're paying for features you don't use and want something simpler at a quarter of the price, let's talk.

**中文：**

> 完全理解 —— 那些都是不错的工具，Birdeye 尤其是。主要的区别：
>
> - Birdeye 月费 $299–$599，还要 $500–$1500 的安装费，要签合同。我们是 $89/月创始价，没有安装费，没有合同。
> - Birdeye 和 Podium 是以英文为主，西班牙文是翻译过去的，中文支持基本都很差。我们从第一天就是按三语言并重设计的。
> - 最重要的是，那些工具到"收集好评"就结束了。BAAM Review 还会接着帮你 —— 网站上的好评 widget、自动生成小红书图片、转介绍链接、营业额归因。这就是为什么我们的客户即使 Birdeye 给他们打 5 折也不回去。
>
> 不勉强 —— Birdeye 用得顺手，价格不是问题，那就继续用。但如果你觉得有些功能用不上又付了钱，想要一个简单的、价格只有四分之一的，咱们可以聊聊。

>
> 1. **Staff Mode** —— 一个屏幕的手机 app，你的前台人员用。开心的患者走了之后，前台点一下按钮，输入顾客名字，短信就发出去了。20 秒搞定，不用学习。

**中文：**

> 模式完全不一样。我们不存好评 —— 好评是 Google 的，存在 Google。我们只是帮你的满意顾客把好评真的发出去。
>
> 我们不藏好评，不压负面好评，不收钱删 1 星（那是 Google 的事，跟我们没关系）。你 Google 上的口碑还是你的，BAAM Review 明天倒闭了，你的好评一条不少还在那。

### Objection 4: "The AI part makes me uncomfortable — it feels fake"

**EN:**

> Honest concern, and we built around it. Two things:
>
> 1. **The customer is always the author.** They answer three questions, AI suggests a draft based on their actual answers, and they edit it before posting. They can rewrite it completely, regenerate it, or ignore the AI suggestion entirely. The review on Google is what they sent — not what the AI wrote.
> 2. **Nothing about the customer is fabricated.** Their name, their email, their visit, their actual feedback — all real. The AI just helps them turn three quick answers into prose they don't have to draft from a blank screen.
>
> Compare it to: a friend types up your dictation. You said the words, the friend wrote them down legibly. That's all the AI is doing.
>
> Google has been very explicit that AI-assisted writing is fine as long as the customer is the author and approves the final content. We're conservative on this — we'd lose our entire business if Google flagged us, so we make sure every guardrail is in place.

**中文：**

> 这个担心很真实，我们就是围绕这个设计的。两点：
>
> 1. **顾客始终是作者。** 他们回答三个问题，AI 根据他们的实际回答给一个草稿建议，他们改完再发。他们可以完全重写、重新生成、或者完全无视 AI 的建议。最后发到 Google 上的，是他们自己发的 —— 不是 AI 写的。
> 2. **关于顾客的信息一个字都不是假的。** 名字、邮箱、来访记录、实际的反馈 —— 全是真的。AI 只是帮他们把三个简短的回答整理成完整的句子，省得他们对着空白页面发愁。
>
> 类比一下：朋友帮你把口述的内容打字。话是你说的，朋友只是帮你写下来。AI 做的就是这个。
>
> Google 明确说过 AI 辅助写作是可以的，只要顾客是作者并且认可最终内容。我们在这点上很保守 —— 如果 Google 把我们标记成违规我们整个生意就完了，所以每个边界我们都守得很紧。

### Objection 5: "I don't want my customers to feel pressured"

**EN:**

> Totally fair, and one of our most important design principles. A few things to know:
>
> - **The SMS is one message, ever.** No follow-up nags, no "we noticed you didn't post a review yet" reminders. One ask, opt-out built in, that's it.
> - **The customer chooses public or private.** Every page has a "share privately with the clinic instead" option that goes directly to your inbox without ever asking for a Google post. We don't gate — every customer sees that option, not just the happy ones.
> - **No incentives or trades.** We don't let you offer discounts for reviews (that's actually against Google's policy and we won't enable it). The customer's review is voluntary; their goodwill is the only currency.
>
> If anything, we err on the side of *under*-asking. Your customer's experience with you is more valuable than any single review.

**中文：**

> 完全理解，这其实是我们最重要的设计原则之一。几点要知道：
>
> - **短信只发一次。** 没有催促的后续短信，没有"我们注意到你还没发好评"的提醒。问一次，附带退订，就这样。
> - **顾客自己选择公开还是私下反馈。** 每个页面都有"私下跟诊所分享"的选项，直接进你的邮箱，完全不会要求他们发 Google。我们不分流 —— 每个顾客都看得到这个选项，不只是开心的顾客。
> - **没有奖励或者交换。** 我们不允许你为了好评提供折扣（这违反 Google 政策，我们也不会做这个功能）。顾客的好评是自愿的；他们对你的好感才是真正的资产。
>
> 如果说有偏向，我们宁可"问得不够"也不会过度问。你跟顾客之间的信任比任何一条好评都值钱。

> 2. 你每个月有多少新顾客？（假设 40 个）

## A few cross-cutting principles

These aren't tactics. They're the underlying posture every conversation should reflect.

### Don't oversell

If someone is paying $499 to Birdeye and getting value out of it, don't try to convince them to switch. They'll churn back within a quarter. Your founding 50 should be people for whom $89/month is genuinely a transformation — not people you talked into it.

### Honor "no"

If someone says no clearly, don't put them in a drip campaign or send four follow-ups. Note the date, file them under "Re-approach Q4," and move on. Their no today might be a yes in six months. Pestering kills that future yes.

### Lead with the wedge, follow with the loop

Tier 1 and most of Tier 2 will buy on the wedge alone ("more Google reviews"). The full Review-to-Revenue loop is what keeps them paying past month 3. Don't open with the full loop pitch — it's too much information up front. Open with "more reviews," let them feel the value, then introduce the rest of the loop in week 2 of their subscription.

### Tell them why $89, not just $99-with-discount

"$89/month for the first 50 customers, locked forever" is more compelling than "10% off." The first frames them as a co-creator; the second frames them as a discount-shopper. Use the first framing.

### Listen for the real concern under the stated concern

"Let me think about it" usually means "I don't see why I need this." Rather than re-pitching, ask: "What's the part you're not sure about — the price, the AI piece, the time commitment, or whether reviews actually drive business for you?" The answer to that question is the conversation you should be having.

### Don't apologize for the price

$89 is a fair price for what BAAM Review does. Birdeye charges $299. NiceJob charges $125. You're priced below both with more features. If you flinch on the price, your customer will flinch too. State it cleanly and move on.

## A final note on tone

The 中文 templates above are deliberately more formal than the EN versions because:
- WeChat business communication norms expect more formality than English-language SMS
- The Chinese-speaking SMB owner demographic skews older and values courtesy in business openers
- "你好" and "您好" are different registers — the templates use "你好" (informal-friendly) because you're trying to build a peer relationship, not a vendor-customer one

If you find a specific customer prefers a more casual or more formal register, mirror their style. The templates are starting points.
